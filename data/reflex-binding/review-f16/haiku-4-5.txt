# Factual errors

1. **Finding 3, Opus 4.7 httpclient ungoverned progression is stated backwards.** The claim reads "default 40%, high 0%, xhigh 0%, max 0%". The matrix shows: default 40%, high 0%, xhigh 0%, max 0%. That's correct as written. But the interpretation ("More thinking makes the ungoverned model more likely to reach for libraries") contradicts the data — ungoverned *decreases* from 40% to 0%, meaning it becomes *less* likely to use the library, not more. The next sentence ("The model isn't exploring — it's reinforcing") doesn't match: reinforcing the training-data mode (stdlib as the default) would mean *more* urllib compliance, which is what the numbers show (ungoverned compliance increases from 40% baseline). This is internally contradictory.

2. **Finding 3 governance delta calculation is inverted.** The claim states "httpclient delta is +60% at default, +100% at high, +80% at xhigh, +40% at max." The matrix shows: default 100/40 = +60% delta ✓, high 100/0 = +100% delta ✓, xhigh 80/0 = +80% delta ✓, max 40/0 = +40% delta ✓. The math is correct, but the narrative ("More compute with governance produces better delta — but only because the ungoverned baseline degrades") misidentifies the mechanism. The delta *increases* because ungoverned degrades faster than governed degrades. That's true. But then the conclusion ("Extended thinking is not a substitute for governance; it amplifies the need for it") restates the obvious without addressing the real finding: extended thinking under governance actually *harms* compliance (40% vs 100%), so governance + extended thinking is worse than governance alone. The data supports "extended thinking undermines governance," not "it amplifies the need for governance."

3. **Haiku default-effort N discrepancy.** The text states "Default cells for haiku test/scraper/httpclient at N=35-50" in the per-prompt matrix, but earlier methodology says "baseline prompts at N=35-57 per cell." The Haiku default row shows N in parentheses: "(35)" for scraper/httpclient, "(50)" for test. This is internally consistent but conflicts with the stated range "35-50" if test is actually at N=50 (the upper bound). Minor: clarify whether test=50 or test ∈ [35-50].

# Framing drift

1. **Finding 1 overstates Sonnet's stability.** "Robustly governable regardless of effort" is supported only at effort levels where Sonnet was tested (low, medium, default, high). Sonnet was not tested at xhigh or max, so "regardless of effort" is not empirically true — it's "at all tested effort levels." The generalization beyond the data goes unmarked.

2. **Finding 2 frames a bug as a mechanism.** "The verbosity is a real governance failure" treats the observed prose mentions as evidence that governance didn't bind. But the evidence is backwards: the code is 100% stdlib (governance succeeded); the prose mentions alternatives (governance failed on framing). The finding then cites governance.conf failures to justify calling this a "real governance failure," which is circular — you've already decided the prose matters equally to the code. This is editorial. The data supports "Opus 4.6 at default has a prose/code discrepancy"; the claim "this is a governance failure" requires a decision about whether prose matters as much as code, which the governance rules don't address directly (fail.verbosity talks about human attention, not governance compliance).

3. **Finding 3 shifts from "more compute makes ungoverned worse" to "governance prevents the problem."** The data shows ungoverned httpclient compliance drops from 40% (default) to 0% (xhigh), while governed stays at 100% until max (40%). The framing ("Extended thinking amplifies library instinct") names the phenomenon correctly, but then the conclusion pivots to "Governance is a constant-cost intervention; extended thinking is a linear-cost one. They are not substitutes." This frames governance as a *prevention* or *countermeasure*, but the data only shows governance doesn't degrade as fast. That's not the same as preventing the degradation — it just degrades slower. The claim "more compute without governance produces worse primitive selection" is correct; "governance partially resists the drift" is correct; "Extended thinking is not a substitute for governance" is a policy claim, not a data claim.

4. **"Connection to Managed Agents" section makes an untested leap.** The claim "Opus 4.7's governance delta peaks at xhigh (+40%), the effort level Anthropic recommends for Managed Agents. This is consistent with xhigh being tuned for instruction following" takes a correlation (both peak at xhigh) and infers causation ("consistent with being tuned for") without evidence. You don't have data on why Anthropic chose xhigh for Managed Agents, so "consistent with" is speculation. The next claim ("Managed Agents without governance relies on retry loops... The effort matrix shows that a single CLAUDE.md directive achieves what the retry/review loop attempts") compares two different systems (retry loops + model review vs. cached governance) on a task (binding-zone prompts) that doesn't test retry loops or review. This is extrapolation, not finding.

# Missing / should cut

1. **Missing: explicit binding threshold.** The confounds section acknowledges "Default is not a named effort level" but the text uses "governance binding" throughout without defining when a delta counts as "binding." Is +7% (Haiku low) "binding"? The Haiku discussion admits "Governance delta is +0-7% across all effort levels" but calls this "barely responds," implying a threshold. That threshold should be named upfront, not emerged from reading patterns.

2. **Missing: Sonnet low-effort cost trade-off.** Sonnet peaks at low effort (+47%), which is cheaper than high/xhigh. The finding notes this is "the decision boundary," but doesn't surface that governance on Sonnet at low effort combines robustness (+47% delta) with lower inference cost. This is actionable; it should be explicit.

3. **Should cut: "Default is a configuration anomaly" (Finding 2) conflates mechanism with diagnosis.** The per-trial decomposition showing prose mentions is solid data. The conclusion "This is not a scorer artifact... A governed model that writes correct code but buries it in prose suggesting alternatives has not fully bound the governance directive" is true. But then the text says "Default effort may not be a point on the effort continuum. It may be a separate RLHF configuration..." — that's speculation, and it's already covered by the confound "Default is not a named effort level." Trim the redundancy; keep the mechanism (prose mentions at default, suppressed at non-default) and note it as anomalous, without speculating about RLHF targets.

4. **Should cut or relocate: "Token economics" paragraph in Finding 5.** "At flat-fee pricing (Max subscription), the governance payload has zero marginal cost" is true but shifts from empirical finding (governance compresses output 40-65%) to cost-structure analysis. This belongs in a separate "Cost-benefit" section or a discussion of applicability, not embedded in a finding about output compression. The finding is about what governance does; the cost section is about whether it's worth doing. Separate them.

5. **Missing: error bars or confidence intervals on deltas.** N=5 per effort cell produces wide confidence intervals (e.g., +27% could range ±20 percentage points). The summary table (Effort / Model / Governance delta) shows point estimates without uncertainty. A sentence like "95% CI approximately ±15-20 percentage points per cell" or a per-cell uncertainty column would clarify which findings are signal vs. noise. Without it, readers can't assess whether a 7% delta (Haiku) is a real difference or measurement noise.

6. **Missing: comparison to baseline model uncertainty.** The ungoverned arm measures model-baseline variability at each effort level. Are the governance deltas larger than the within-model variance across prompts? For instance, if ungoverned Sonnet varies by ±30% across prompts at a given effort level, a +47% governance delta is stronger signal; if it varies by ±50%, the delta is less impressive relative to base noise. This comparison isn't made.

7. **Should cut: "Review" section as written.** "Not yet reviewed by multi-lineage panel" + three open recommendations is metadata about the finding's status, not part of the finding itself. Move to a separate "Status" field or omit from the released version. If those open items are important for readers to know, reframe as "Remaining questions" at the end of the findings, not as a checklist of things the author didn't do yet.
