## 2. FRAMING DRIFT

**"The governance artifact transfers discipline, not knowledge" (Mechanism section):**
This dichotomy (discipline vs knowledge) is interpretive. Behavior change could also be framed as "redirecting attention to known alternatives" or "deprioritizing frameworks." The distinction you're drawing isn't empirically grounded. Reframe as: "governance changes relative preference for existing knowledge, not what the model knows."

**"This is the distillation boundary" (end of Mechanism section):**
Framing the lineage-dependent binding as *the* distillation boundary leans heavily on YouTube videos (Tk0sRTX-zCA, LCXhO6YTBTU) and the Akamai cache analogy as justification. The data shows binding patterns across lineages, but calling it *the* boundary is editorializing. Say instead: "This suggests a distillation-relevant mechanism: lineage determines whether abstract governance transfers" — softer, grounded in your data.

**Akamai analogy (Mechanism section, last paragraph):**
"Not just cache size but cache architecture determines whether the routing table entry resolves" — this metaphor is creative but unmoored from the data. You're observing model behavior, not cache or routing systems. Either show the analogy maps precisely to your measurements or cut it. The core claim (lineage not size) is strong enough without the metaphor.

**"Senior engineer answer" and stdlib preference (Task section):**
The framing that stdlib is the preferred answer relies on "the video that motivated this experiment" without justifying why that norm is the correct one to test. It's fine to test it, but don't frame it as obvious. Say: "We tested whether governance binds on a task with a less-obvious stdlib solution" rather than asserting "the senior engineer answer."

**"Frontier models...the reflex is redundant" (Claim section, line 1):**
Saying the reflex is "redundant because the knowledge is already in their weights" attributes a cause (they already have the knowledge) that's not testable here. The reflex *appears* redundant behaviorally (same outputs both ways), but you're inferring the mechanism. Say: "frontier models already use stdlib regardless of governance; binding is unobservable against the ungoverned baseline."
