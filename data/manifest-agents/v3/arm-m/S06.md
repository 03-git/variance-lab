**emile.txt** is a deployment pattern — a concrete specification of how to instantiate subtract on real hardware for a specific use case (sole proprietor, one device, one model). It names primitives (SSH authorized_keys, /Users/Shared/, signing keys), specifies what the substrate commits to and refuses to commit to, and ends with federation and verification steps.

**subtract.7.txt** is the man-page specification — the conceptual frame. It defines what subtract *is* at the discipline level: "a translation layer between human intent and the shell," serving two populations (humans and agents), operating across three layers (subtract, human, agent). It doesn't name hardware or users.

The relationship is **specification → instantiation**:

- `subtract.7.txt` defines the three-layer subtraction model and the two populations abstractly. It says subtract "has no binary of its own at the computer layer" and is "a discipline loaded into an existing shell session."
- `emile.txt` operationalizes that discipline for a particular context. Where `subtract.7.txt` says the human subtracts from the agent's context, `emile.txt` expresses that as five named primitives (admin principal, user principals, shared directory, signing model, SSH keys) — all still Unix, none naming a role or org.

`emile.txt` also extends the specification in one direction `subtract.7.txt` doesn't reach: **federation**. When a user outgrows the operator's substrate, they become an operator of their own instance — two sovereign substrates exchanging signed artifacts. That's not in the man page; it's a deployment-layer consequence of the substrate's signing primitive.

The lineage paragraph in `emile.txt` ("Porat, Fox, Ramey, Rousseau") mirrors the abstraction in `subtract.7.txt` but grounds it: Rousseau's name is on both the deployment pattern and the node (`rousseau`) running it, which is the man page's principle — "the environment teaches, not the instructor" — made physical.
